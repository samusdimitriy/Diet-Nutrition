Glossary
  1. Whole-food: Food that is minimally processed and free from additives or artificial substances.
  2. Processed-food: Food that has undergone changes from its natural state.
  3. Animal-based: Food sources derived from animals, including meat, organs, fruit, honey, and raw dairy.
  4. Grass-fed: Animal food sources, like cows and sheep, that consume grass and forage for a portion of their lives before being finished on a grain-based diet.
  5. Grass-finished: Animal food sources, like cows and sheep, that only consume grass and forage throughout their lives, except for milk before weaning.
  6. Pasture-raised: Animal food sources, like poultry and pigs, that consume a significant portion of their nutrition from managed pastures and have more outdoor space to roam and feed.
  7. Wild-caught: Animal food sources, like seafood, that are caught in natural environments such as oceans, rivers, and lakes.

Recovery

As an athlete, recovery is an essential component of your training regimen. It is during the recovery phase that your body repairs and rebuilds itself, allowing you to perform at your best and avoid injuries. Recovery is not just about resting; it involves a combination of strategies to promote healing, reduce muscle soreness, and enhance overall performance.

One of the key elements of recovery is adequate rest. This means getting enough sleep to allow your body to recharge and repair itself. Sleep is when your muscles and tissues undergo repair and growth, and it is also crucial for mental and emotional well-being. Aim for 7-9 hours of quality sleep each night to support your recovery process.

In addition to sleep, nutrition plays a vital role in recovery. Consuming a balanced diet that includes a variety of nutrient-dense foods is essential for optimal recovery. Protein is particularly important for athletes, as it helps repair and rebuild muscle tissue. Carbohydrates replenish glycogen stores, which are depleted during exercise, while healthy fats provide energy and support hormone production. Hydration is also crucial, as it aids in nutrient delivery and waste removal.

Active recovery is another strategy that athletes can utilize to enhance their recovery process. This involves engaging in low-intensity activities such as stretching, yoga, or light cardio exercises. These activities promote blood flow to the muscles, helping to flush out metabolic waste and reduce muscle soreness. Active recovery also helps to maintain flexibility and prevent muscle imbalances.

Incorporating recovery techniques such as massage, foam rolling, and stretching can also be beneficial for athletes. These techniques help to release muscle tension, improve flexibility, and increase blood circulation. They can be particularly useful for targeting specific areas of soreness or tightness.

Lastly, mental and emotional recovery is just as important as physical recovery. High-intensity training and competition can take a toll on your mental well-being, so it's essential to prioritize activities that help you relax and destress. This may include practices such as meditation, mindfulness, or engaging in hobbies and activities that bring you joy.

As an athlete, understanding and implementing effective recovery strategies is crucial for maximizing your performance and preventing injuries. By prioritizing rest, nutrition, active recovery, and mental well-being, you can ensure that your body is in the best possible condition to excel in your chosen sport. Remember, recovery is not a luxury but a necessity for every athlete striving to reach their full potential.

In addition to the strategies mentioned earlier, sauna and ice bath are two popular recovery methods that many athletes incorporate into their routines.

Sauna

Sauna sessions can be beneficial for athletes as they promote relaxation, improve circulation, and aid in muscle recovery. Spending time in a sauna helps to increase blood flow to the muscles, which can help reduce muscle soreness and accelerate the removal of metabolic waste products. The heat from the sauna also helps to relax tight muscles and promote a sense of calm and well-being.

I recommend a sauna 2-3 times per week for 5-20 minutes each.

Cold Exposure

Ice baths, on the other hand, involve immersing your body in cold water or using ice packs to reduce inflammation and promote recovery. The cold temperature helps to constrict blood vessels, which can reduce swelling and inflammation in the muscles. Ice baths are commonly used after intense workouts or competitions to help reduce muscle soreness and speed up the recovery process. It's important to note that ice baths should be used with caution and for a limited duration to avoid potential risks such as hypothermia or skin damage.

I recommend 11 minutes of Cold Exposure per week. You can split it into 2-4 sessions of 1-5 min each.

Both sauna and ice bath can be effective recovery tools, but it's essential to listen to your body and consult with a professional before incorporating them into your routine. Every athlete is unique, and what works for one person may not work for another. Experimenting with different recovery methods and finding what works best for you is key to optimizing your recovery as an athlete.

Eating Principles

Given the ongoing debate around nutrition, I have identified five eating principles that are widely accepted and believed to enhance physical and mental performance. By following these principles, I truly believe that you will be ahead of 99% of the population in terms of health, well-being, and longevity.

  1. Prioritize Whole, Unprocessed Foods: The first and most important step you can take is to minimize or eliminate processed foods from your diet. By choosing whole, unprocessed foods, you provide your body with essential nutrients like vitamins, minerals, fiber, and antioxidants.
  2. Opt for Grass-fed, Pasture-Raised, and Wild-Caught Foods: While these foods may be more expensive, they offer higher nutritional value, are environmentally sustainable, and are ethically sourced. Consider every dollar spent on these foods as an investment in your health, leading to improved performance, mental clarity, and well-being.
  3. Avoid Grains, Simple Carbohydrates, and Sugar: Foods like grains, simple carbohydrates, and sugar can cause spikes in blood sugar levels, leading to a lack of sustained energy throughout the day. They are also high in calories with minimal nutrient content and are known to contribute to inflammation and digestive issues.
  4. Practice Time-Restricted Feeding: To reduce calorie intake and allow your body sufficient time to digest and process food, I recommend following a 16/8 plan at least 2-3 times per week. This involves consuming all your meals within an 8-hour window and fasting for the remaining 16 hours. Or a 24 hour fast 1-2 times per week. With a 24-hour fast, you eat your last meal at 6 pm, for example, and then don't eat your first meal until 6 pm the next day.
  5. Embrace Mindful Eating: If you struggle with overeating, I suggest slowing down and paying attention to your hunger and fullness cues. Engage in mindful eating by avoiding distractions like watching TV or working while eating. This will help you better recognize when you're satisfied and prevent overconsumption.

By incorporating these principles into your lifestyle, you can improve your overall health, well-being, and longevity.

Supplements

Supplements can be a helpful addition to one's diet, especially when it becomes challenging to obtain all the necessary nutrients through regular food intake. They can help address specific deficiencies or support specific health needs. However, it is always advisable to strive for a balanced diet and aim to cover as many nutrients as possible through natural sources. A diverse and balanced diet consisting of fresh foods usually provides all the essential nutrients that the body requires. Therefore, if possible, I recommend relying on nutrition as the primary source of nutrients and considering supplements as additional support.

How to Eat Out
  1. Check the menu ahead of time and focus on options that align with your usual home-cooked meals or healthier choices.
  2. Choose restaurants that offer grilled proteins, salads, and vegetable dishes, while avoiding fried and highly processed foods.
  3. Control portion sizes by asking the restaurant about serving sizes before ordering.
  4. Prioritize lean proteins like red meat, fish, and chicken.
  5. Include steamed or fresh vegetables to add volume and nutrients to your meal.
  6. Choose cooking methods like steaming, grilling, or baking over frying.
  7. Opt for water, coconut water, unsweetened tea, or low-calorie beverages.
  8. Limit dressings, sauces, and condiments to reduce calorie intake.
  9. Avoid filling up on bread before your meal.
  10. Make small modifications to your order, such as swapping fries for a side of salad or steamed vegetables.
  11. Enjoy dessert in moderation, considering sharing it with others at the table.

By following these tips, you can enjoy dining out while still prioritizing your health.
